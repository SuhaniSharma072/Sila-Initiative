The Sila
Initiative

We are Shrika, Saanvi, and Suhani, three high school students based in the Dallas–Fort Worth area and founders of the Sila Initiative, a student-led effort to develop affordable, high-efficiency air purification systems for remote Alaskan Native communities.

Many rural Alaskan communities experience elevated indoor concentrations of fine particulate matter (PM2.5) due to a combination of wildfire smoke, wood-burning stoves used for home heating, diesel generators, and other combustion-related sources. Prolonged exposure to PM2.5 has been linked to increased risks of asthma, cardiovascular disease, respiratory infections, and other serious health conditions. Because many of these communities are geographically isolated, commercial air purifiers are often expensive to purchase and ship, limiting access to effective indoor air filtration.

To help address this challenge, we have designed a Corsi–Rosenthal cube integrated with real-time particulate matter sensing and air quality forecasting. Our goal is to provide an affordable, energy-efficient solution that can help reduce indoor PM2.5 exposure while remaining practical and accessible for remote communities. We are currently seeking funding to expand prototype development, conduct additional testing, and deploy more units where they are needed most.

The Build

The Design Corsi–Rosenthal Cube Purifier

A Corsi–Rosenthal cube: MERV-13 furnace filters taped into a cube around a fan array, drawing room air through the filters on every side. It's a well-documented, open-source design with no proprietary parts, and simple enough to assemble at a community workshop, so replacement parts don't depend on a long freight chain. We're also building in a particulate sensor and microcontroller so each unit can track and forecast indoor PM2.5 in real time.

Our working prototype: dual-fan cube with MERV-13 filter panels and a sensor module wired in.
Materials Some Materials Each Unit Uses
  • 4 MERV-13 furnace filter/ True HEPA panels, 20"×20"
  • 2 PC fans for airflow
  • PMS5003 particulate sensor
  • ESP32 or Raspberry Pi Pico W running the forecasting model
  • Red, Yellow, Green LEDs for status indication
  • USB power cables and wall adapters
Why Remote Alaska Indoor Air Quality
Ongoing research

Many remote Alaska Native communities heat with wood or oil stoves in tightly sealed homes, and sit downwind of boreal wildfire smoke for days each season. Retail HEPA purifiers and their replacement filters are often costly and slow to arrive given freight distances, which is the gap this project is trying to close.

Partnerships We're Pursuing
